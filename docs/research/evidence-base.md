  Four independent meta-analyses converge on a medium effect (g = 0.50–0.61) for
   retrieval practice vs. restudy. The effect is larger with feedback (g = 0.73
  vs. 0.39 without; Rowland 2014), at longer retention intervals (g = 0.69 at
  24+ hours), and on free recall (g = 0.79) vs. recognition tests (g = 0.32;
  Adesope et al. 2017).

  Transfer

  Pan & Rickard (2018), Psychological Bulletin, 67 articles, 192 transfer ESs, N
   = 10,382: d = 0.40 [0.31, 0.50] for transfer to new questions/contexts.
  Retrieval practice genuinely aids transfer, not just verbatim recall, though
  the transfer effect is smaller than the retention effect.

  Boundary Conditions (Where It Fails)

  - Worked examples for problem-solving: van Gog & Sweller (2015), Educational
  Psychology Review — argued the testing effect disappears with high
  element-interactivity material. Karpicke & Aue (2015), same journal, disputed
  this as overstated. The current consensus: for novel
  procedural/problem-solving skills in novice learners, retrieval practice's
  advantage over worked examples shrinks or vanishes (Yeo & Fazio, 2019, Journal
   of Educational Psychology).
  - Mathematical word problems: Huang, Zheng, Yu & Chen (2023), Frontiers in
  Psychology — null results for retrieval practice vs. restudy on math word
  problems across multiple experiments.
  - Far transfer with mismatched cues: Pan & Rickard (2018) noted transfer
  effects weaken when practice cues are poorly aligned with final test cues.

  AI-Generated Retrieval Practice

  - Bastani et al. (2025), PNAS, ~1,000 students: GPT-4 with Socratic guardrails
   (forcing retrieval) improved practice performance 127% over control but
  showed no gain on unassisted exams. GPT-4 without guardrails harmed exam
  performance by 17%.
  - LLM-generated MCQs in data science courses (2025, arXiv 2507.05629,
  peer-reviewed venue): ~60 students, 89% vs. 73% quiz accuracy with
  LLM-generated retrieval questions.
  - LLM vs. expert anatomy MCQs (2025, Medical Education Online): No significant
   quality differences across 17 criteria between ChatGPT/Gemini-generated and
  expert-authored items.

  Key implication: AI can generate psychometrically sound retrieval practice
  questions. But the Bastani finding is critical — AI must enforce retrieval
  (give hints, not answers) or it substitutes for the very effort that produces
  the learning benefit.

  2. Spaced Repetition

  Evidence for Optimal Intervals

  Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted & Rohrer (2006), Psychological Bulletin, 184
  articles, 839 assessments: The spacing effect is robust (d ≈ 0.85 for verbal
  recall per later characterizations), but there is no single optimal interval —
   the ISI-to-retention-interval ratio matters.

  Cepeda, Vul, Rohrer, Wixted & Pashler (2008), Psychological Science, N =
  1,354: The optimal gap-to-test-delay ratio declines from ~20–40% for a 1-week
  test to ~5–10% for a 1-year test. For a 1-year retention target, the optimal
  review gap was ~3–5 weeks.

  Conceptual Understanding (Not Just Facts)

  - Vlach & Sandhofer (2012), Child Development, N = 36 children: Spacing
  improved generalization of food chain concepts — partial η² = .478 (very
  large) for complex generalization.
  - Kapler, Weston & Wiseheart (2015), Learning and Instruction, N = 169
  undergraduates: Spaced review improved both factual and higher-level
  application questions on a 5-week delayed test.

  At Scale in Real Classrooms

  Lindsey, Shroyer, Pashler & Mozer (2014), Psychological Science, N = 179
  eighth-graders, full semester: Personalized spacing algorithm produced a 16.5%
   boost over massed review and 10% over generic spacing on a cumulative exam 1
  month post-semester. For early-semester material, the gain was worth ~2 letter
   grades.

  Barzagar Nazari & Ebersbach (2025), Behavioral Sciences, 22 classroom studies,
   N > 3,000: Overall d = 0.54 [0.31, 0.77]. Language learning d = 0.91; math d
  = 0.46.

  ALEKS in math (2021 meta-analysis, Investigations in Mathematics Learning,
  9,238 students): g = 0.05 as replacement for instruction; g = 0.43 as
  supplement. Spacing-based adaptive systems work better as supplements, not
  replacements.

  Spacing and Motivation

  This is the evidence base's weakest point:

  - Kornell & Bjork (2008), Psychological Science, N = 120: Spacing vastly
  outperformed massing on inductive learning (61% vs. 35%), yet 72% of
  participants judged massing as more effective. Students consistently misjudge
  spacing as inferior.
  - Kornell (2009), Applied Cognitive Psychology: Spacing was more effective for
   90% of participants in GRE vocab study, yet 72% believed massing worked
  better.
  - No large-scale RCTs measuring dropout/attrition from spaced practice
  interventions were found. The literature acknowledges adherence as a barrier
  but does not quantify it rigorously.

  This is a significant gap. The metacognitive illusion (massing feels more
  effective) means any system relying on spacing must overcome student
  resistance or operate without requiring student buy-in.

  3. The Self-Reference Effect

  Original Finding

  Rogers, Kuiper & Kirker (1977), Journal of Personality and Social Psychology,
  N = 59: Participants rated trait adjectives under structural, phonemic,
  semantic, or self-reference ("Describes you?") conditions. Self-reference
  produced the highest recall — roughly 2x semantic encoding. No standardized
  effect size reported in the original.

  Meta-Analytic Confirmation

  Symons & Johnson (1997), Psychological Bulletin, k = 129 studies: d = 0.65
  (self-reference vs. semantic encoding). Effect larger for free recall (~0.47)
  than recognition (~0.93). The SRE works because the self is a well-organized,
  frequently-used cognitive structure that promotes both elaboration and
  organization.

  Replications

  - Bentley et al. (2017), PLOS ONE, N = 658 across 4 online studies: d = 0.69,
  closely replicating the meta-analytic estimate.
  - Crump Lab (Brooklyn College): Replicated the SRE in no-practice conditions,
  but found the SRE disappeared when retrieval practice was equalized across all
   conditions — suggesting the SRE may partly operate via spontaneous
  self-testing during self-referential encoding.

  Application to Educational Content

  This literature is surprisingly thin:

  - Hartlep & Forsyth (2000), Teaching of Psychology: College students studied a
   child psychology textbook chapter using self-referencing. Enhanced retention
  at 2 weeks vs. control. No effect size reported.
  - Turk et al. (2015), Learning and Instruction, N = 47 + 32 children:
  Self-referent sentence generation improved spelling accuracy by ~20%.
  - Pruss, Karni & Prior (2025), Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, N = 42 + 40:
  Self-referencing for foreign vocabulary — d = 0.71 (immediate), d = 0.52
  (1-week delay). This is the strongest evidence for educationally-relevant SRE
  across sessions with a delayed test.

  Limits

  - Saturated self-schema: Jackson et al. (2018), Psychological Neuroscience, N
  = 153: After 10 minutes of self-focused writing, the SRE was eliminated (η²
  dropped from .32 to non-significant).
  - Collectivist cultures: Zhu et al. (2007): East Asian participants showed
  equivalent memory for self- and close-other-referenced information; Western
  participants showed the standard self > other pattern.
  - Retrieval practice may subsume it: If the SRE operates via spontaneous
  retrieval, then a system already implementing explicit retrieval practice may
  see reduced incremental benefit from self-reference.

  Adding interesting but irrelevant information hurts learning. The damage is
  worse for transfer than retention, worse for images (g = -0.95) than text (g =
   -0.27), and worse when placed near relevant diagrams.

  The Critical Distinction: Personalization ≠ Seductive Details

  Mayer's Personalization Principle: Conversational style ("you/your" instead of
   formal third person) — median d = 1.11 across 11/11 experiments on transfer
  tests (Moreno & Mayer, 2000, 2004). This personalizes the relevant content
  itself without adding irrelevant material.

  Walkington & Bernacki (2018), International Journal of Artificial Intelligence
   in Education, N = 151 Algebra I students: Distinguished surface
  personalization (changing topic words to match interests) from deep
  personalization (incorporating students' actual quantitative experiences).
  Deep personalization improved learning and reduced gaming — but only when
  matched to genuine engagement with the interest domain.

  Design implication: Personalize how relevant content is framed (conversational
   tone, self-referential prompts, interest-aligned problem contexts). Do not
  add interesting-but-tangential anecdotes, images, or stories.

  No published RCT of autonomous LLM tutoring shows positive results on delayed 
  tests. This is the single most important gap in the evidence base. The Kestin
  study (the one positive result) measured only immediately and used Harvard    
  students. The two studies measuring delayed outcomes (Bastani, Barcaui) both
  found harm from unrestricted AI access.

  Historical Failures at Scale                                                  
  
  - Knewton: $180M+ raised, no published peer-reviewed evidence, acquired by    
  Wiley at a fraction of funding.                                 
  - Carnegie Learning Cognitive Tutor: RAND RCT (Pane et al., 2014, Educational 
  Evaluation and Policy Analysis), 147 schools, 24,262 students — Year 1: null; 
  Year 2 high school only: d = 0.20; middle school: null.
  - ALEKS: Meta-analysis (2021, Investigations in Mathematics Learning), 9,238  
  students — g = 0.05 as replacement; g = 0.43 as supplement only.              
  
  ---                                                                           
  Synthesis: What the Evidence Actually Supports                  
                                                                                
  Strong evidence FOR the component mechanisms:
  - Retrieval practice: g = 0.50–0.61, including transfer (d = 0.40). Robust    
  across 4 meta-analyses.                                                       
  - Spaced repetition: d = 0.54–0.85. Works in real classrooms. Personalized    
  scheduling outperforms generic spacing by 10%.                                
  - Self-reference encoding: d = 0.65 (meta-analytic). Replicates reliably. But 
  thin evidence in educational contexts specifically.                          
  - Avoiding seductive details while personalizing: Supported by both the       
  coherence principle (d = -0.33 for seductive details) and the personalization
  principle (d = 1.11 for conversational framing).                              
                                                                  
  Unresolved or concerning evidence:                                            
  1. No published RCT of LLM-based tutoring shows durable learning gains on 
  delayed tests. The two delayed-test studies found harm from unrestricted AI.  
  2. The self-reference effect may be subsumed by retrieval practice — if you're
   already implementing retrieval, the incremental benefit of self-reference is 
  unknown.                                                                      
  3. Spacing works but students hate it — 72% judge it as inferior to massing.
  Compliance in self-directed contexts is the unsolved problem.                 
  4. AI must enforce retrieval effort (Bastani et al.) — any system that makes  
  answers too easy substitutes for the cognitive work that produces learning. 
  5. Historical ITS scaled poorly: Carnegie Learning's d = 0.20 at scale was far
   below lab estimates; ALEKS as a replacement was null; Knewton produced no    
  evidence at all.                                                              
                                                                  
  The hypothesis is theoretically well-grounded but empirically unproven as a   
  combined system. Each component has strong individual evidence. The           
  combination has never been tested in a single RCT with delayed outcomes. The
  largest risk is that AI access, even with good intentions, reduces the        
  desirable difficulty that makes retrieval practice and spacing effective —
  exactly the mechanism documented by Bastani et al. (2025) and Barcaui (2025).
